Design Critique:

The visualization encodes the weeks of each year in polar coordinates, with the years 2020–2022 represented as a spiral timeline. The spiral line shows the progression of each year, and the area inside the red band sitting on the line represents the number of new COVID cases, averaged across each week. This encoding makes seasonal patterns easy to see, and the trend between 2020 to 2022 appears salient. The radial layout also makes it relatively clear where to begin reading; however it is unclear why the spiral starts at an arbitrary location — it could begin at center to better draw attention and adhere to conventions for spirals. The spiral expanding outwards also helps showcase a sense of the impending future. However, visualizing this data as a spiral in polar coordinates can also be confusing in many ways. While it highlights broad seasonal patterns, it makes comparisons of COVID cases across different months (e.g. March and July) or entire years (2021 vs 2020) more difficult because differences in area are harder to perceptually differentiate when they are not right above one another. A standard line chart or small multiples by year might make month-to-month comparisons clearer while still preserving seasonal patterns. In addition, when reading yearly trends the layout can be slightly misleading: because 2020 is inside the 2021 spiral, the 2021 line is longer, which may cause the viewer to think the designer is exaggerating the timeline and the number of cases in 2021 (due to area on the outside being larger than the inside for the same numerical values).
Area bands around a line are associated with the convention of showing uncertainty of some value so using these bands to encode the 7-day average also threw me off a little bit. I would try to pick a different mark which does not look similar to an uncertainty band and could be useful (maybe a line centered around 0). On this subject, showing uncertainty or variation in the data is not necessary but if the average value was not encoded in another mark besides area, it could be useful to show the variation within each week across all 7 days. The spiral line also does not appear to encode additional information besides acting as a timeline and therefore seems somewhat redundant (as the timeline already implicitly communicated with the spiral of the area mark.
I take the target audience to be the average U.S. citizen reading the article, and I think the visualization generally communicates broad trends in COVID cases and emphasizes seasonal effects by “stacking” or rather spiraling years on top of months. Here, I think adding seasonal markers directly along the spiral would make temporal orientation clearer and reduce the cognitive effort required to interpret the timeline and allow people to better compare seasonal trends. Overall, while the unconventional data encoding to the spiral and the polar coordinates of the chart draw attention, they may add confusion to the interpretation. The takeaway was also a bit unclear since I’m not sure exactly how I should think about increases in covid cases — is it due to seasonal factors only or is there something more? Why should we expect even greater and greater increases seasons over season? While most elements are reasonably labeled, I think legend is poorly designed since it doesn't showcase what the maximum value of new COVID-19 cases would look like and the wording inside makes it seem like daily COVID-19 cases are being shown rather than the 7-week average. I also wished the annotation would point to specific events to provide meaningful interpretation of why such trends might be occurring.

Visualization Sketches

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • I wanted to try to keep monthly/seasonal comparisons accessible for new cases while also allowing the viewer to visually perceive yearly trends across each year. This would also allow a comparison of year trends. I also wanted to use a more common convention so it would make parsing the chart a bit easier.
  • By encoding the number of new cases in a line, I hoped this chart would encourage people to consider the area beneath the line and compare it across seasons. I wanted people to take away that while the area during the summer months is low and only marginally different, the winter months are where the trends really matter, showing clear year-over-year differences. To highlight this message, I also added an annotation emphasizing the differences at the end of the year.
  • I think this chart did a great job roughly showing yearly trends and showing broad differences in seasonal trends. However, I think the exact week-over-week difference between years is not prominent and so it's hard to compare when looking back and forth between two different charts. In the next chart, I want to remove the facets and combined information in one chart to highlight larger differences year-over-year relative to the minor differences.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • This time, I wanted to make the differences between years more prominent and to highlight the large differences between the winter seasons of 2021 and 2022 more explicitly. I also wanted to make comparisons between years easier and more precise.
  • By creating layered charts and shading regions where cases were higher than in the previous year, I hoped to address the aforementioned issues and make comparisons of weekly differences between years easier.
  • Here, I think that following the lines between different years may have been somewhat confusing, so adding color to the lines may be useful. While I felt the emphasis on differences was clear, simply showing the large gaps in cases through the colored areas between lines was too conventional and did not effectively convey how significant the differences were, and the impact of each case. In the next sketch, I want to emphasize the importance of every new case across all weeks.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • For this sketch, I wanted to clearly show the impact of each case through a slightly more unconventional visualization to evoke emotion. While I think showing trends across the months and precise differences between weeks is important, I also wanted to emphasize the gravity of having significantly more cases.
  • By drawing a bubble for each week and encoding its size as the number of cases, I hoped to communicate that 2022 not only had higher COVID-19 case counts during the winter compared to other years, but also that they were much higher than in previous months. I also hoped that the quantity of circles (one circle per week) and their sizes would evoke a stronger sense of impact while still somewhat following conventions. While I wanted to make things very unconventional like the original chart which had some emotional impact — I was reminded that the original chart may be considered too unconventional in a way that hinders engagement.
  • While I think the size and quantity of the circles convey more emotion, I still think faceting by year makes comparisons between months more difficult to follow. Although the annotations help draw attention to differences in circle sizes and seasons, a layered approach might be more effective for comparing sizes, perhaps while encoding year using color and opacity. In the final design, I want to explore encoding information with color and opacity, such that yearly trends can be overlaid while also drawing emphasis on seasonal differences.

Final Visualization Design

Here, I was looking to emphasize not only the average number of new COVID-19 cases across different years like the original visualization, and show seasonal and future trends, but also to show the death toll relative to each new case. I did this to both emotionally ground the consequences of each new COVID-19 case but also showcase the impact of preventive and medical measures year-over-year. I used the circle mark to encode the 7-day average for new COVID-19 cases, representing one circle for each week because it helps visually perceive linear trends over and between weeks and years. It also provides slightly less clutter than drawing out a line. I used color to encode different years so a comparison across seasons and years could be made, however one problem which this created was that it was hard to see overlapping circles. To solve this, I lowered the opacity of all circles such that overlaps were obvious. All colors were chosen to be different kinds of grey hues because I wanted to emphasize the gravity and consequences of each case through darker colors associated with death and sickness. I encoded the number of deaths as the size of the circles. I did this to showcase how height (number of new cases) correlated with number deaths, across each year and to really drive home the emotional impact of each case. Specifically to show how health and hospital infrastructure and accessibility was lagging behind in 2021 as compared to 2022, which might have led to more deaths per case. I also highlight the background different shades of red, showcasing different seasons, with the opacity being relative to the average number of deaths in that season. This made sure to emphasize the trend of the drastic cases and deaths in the winter time. While information in this visualization showcases death as a consequence of getting diagnosed with COVID-19, it perhaps frames this as the only significant consequence. There are many other tragic outcomes which are not covered, like the number of hospitalizations, permanent health issues, financial damage, which might drive a significant burden on individuals and families which are not shown inside this visualization.
On a side note: while aggregating the data for the number of new cases in 2022, I noticed that there were a few outliers. There was no data between week 38 to 51 but there was data for week 52. I also found a day with negative values. Due to these reasons, during the data processing stage, I omitted those weeks from my visualization.
I think that the final design addresses most of the critiques I surfaced in Step 2. I was able to remove the confusing area bands with circle marks that more clearly represent the 7-day average and reduce visual clutter. Removing the spiral and polar coordinates also simplifies the timeline and makes the chart easier to interpret, while encoding deaths as circle size adds meaningful context about the severity of COVID cases across years, which was missing. I also tried annotations to emphasize certain trends and points of importance. The design process helped me try out a different set of marks and strategies to emphasize seasonal and yearly differences. I think I was able to incorporate the lessons from all of the prior designs, specifically about showcasing linear trends from designs 1 and 2, showcasing differences by shading (does with opacity in the final sketch) from design 2, and showcasing impact and consequences via circle mark and size from design 3. Something I was not able to incorporate was communicating the notion of variation in the data as it would add to the visual clutter and the circle marks do not come with strong conventions for showcasing variation or uncertainty. Overall, this process made me realize that not all marks and channels come with the same baggage. While I was able to encode size within the circle mark and make my visualization “easy” to read due to cultural conventions, it would not have been true if I chose another shape like line.
