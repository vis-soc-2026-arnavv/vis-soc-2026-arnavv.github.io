Subtheme: Zoning and housing supply

Overall Analysis Questions

Single-family zoning plays a major role in shaping housing supply, affordability, and patterns of inequality, and it is frequently criticized as an exclusionary policy that limits access to housing for low-income and minority communities (Washington Post: Houses are too expensive. Apartments are too small. Is this a fix?). Reporting on the Massachusetts housing crisis highlights statewide affordability pressures ( Boston Globe Spotlight Report into the Massachusetts housing crisis) but articles on like City Observatory: The Illegal City of Somerville focus on how restrictive zoning operates in specific municipalities. these accounts often emphasize either the state as a whole or individual case studies, leaving open the question of how single-family zoning varies across municipalities and whether its impacts differ by local context. Therefore, I wabt ti examine how single-family zoning is distributed across Massachusetts, whether it follows geographic patterns (e.g., urban versus suburban or rural areas), and whether zoning share is associated with demographic composition or population size. In doing so, I also want to investigate which municipalities exhibit the largest differences between single-family and other residential zoning areas and whether income or demographic patterns systematically vary with zoning share. Thus, my questions are:
  1. How is single-family zoning distributed across Massachusett's municipalities?
  2. Is there a relationship between single-family zoning share and demographic composition (age, race/ethnicity, gender)?
  3. Is there a relationship between municipality population size and the share of single-family zoning?
  4. Which municipalities end up with the biggest differences between single-family zoned and other housing zoned areas?
  5. Do demographic or income patterns systematically differ by single-family zoning share?

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

Single-family zoning in Massachusetts is unevenly distributed across municipalities, with many eastern and coastal cities (for example, Boston)showing little to no single-family zoning, while more rural and western municipalities (for example, Dover and Bolton) have very high shares. However, neighboring towns can adopt very different zoning policies, meaning that geography alone does not determine zoning patterns. There does not appear to be a clear relationship between municipality population size and single-family zoning share, although inconsistencies and sampling limitations in the dataset make precise comparisons difficult. At the municipal level, demographic composition (age, race/ethnicity, and gender) does not show a strong correlation with the share of single-family zoning, though some municipalities with lower single-family zoning tend to be more racially diverse and slightly older along the eastern coast. Within municipalities, some notable differences emerge: places like Newton and Wellesley show substantial racial shifts between single-family and other zoning areas, with single-family zones having much higher shares of non-Hispanic White residents. Finally, income patterns do not systematically track with zoning share across the state, but some wealthier municipalities such as Needham, Medfield, and Wellesley tend to have both higher incomes and substantial single-family zoning, while lower-income cities like Chelsea and Winthrop have little single-family zoning overall. Specfically, I found the case of Wellesley to be particularly interesting as has such high disparitiy in demographics between single-family and other zoning areas but also a high income level.
